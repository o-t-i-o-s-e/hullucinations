His Slack status icon was always lit green.
Whenever management dropped a new milestone into the company-wide channel, he was the first to smash the "🚀" and "🔥" reaction emojis. He would mentally fill in the gaps of the rough specifications thrown over by the Product Manager (PM), break them down into tickets, and assign them to the engineers.

At night, he would send lengthy DMs to engineers who were lagging behind. "Hey, did you confirm the release schedule with the SRE team for this implementation? We just had a security incident the other day, so I think it's risky to push forward with this rough code. If you'd like, I can give a heads-up to the senior architect, and we can sync up in a 1-on-1 first?"

He was the tech lead supporting a fast-growing startup in Tokyo.
Management praised him at every opportunity. "Development wouldn't run without you. You are the culture of this company itself," they told him.
From the system architecture to the history of scrapped specifications, and even the idiosyncratic quirks of their clients—every piece of implicit knowledge was stored exclusively within his brain. He prided himself on being the most critical "Single Point of Failure" in this organization. If he went down, the system would stop. That crushing pressure was his identity; it was his very reason for living.

However, he justified it under the banner of "Overwhelming Ownership," continuing to over-adapt while grinding himself down. "If the AI's accuracy has gotten this high, we can reduce the headcount of mid-level engineers next quarter. It'll cut down on useless communication costs and increase development transparency. Up until now, people had to ask questions on Slack, but now anyone can understand everything just by looking at the GitHub Issues. This is awesome!"
It was an extremely rational thought process, reflecting shareholder pressure and global trends. Unconsciously, he reacted to that sterile declaration with a "💯" emoji.

"We know you're trying to be considerate by scheduling your mentions to send exactly at 9:00 AM when the workday starts. But everyone knows your status has been glowing green since 5:00 AM."
Everyone understands your kindness and sense of responsibility, but could you perhaps take a step back and adopt a more hands-off stance?
It was a notice that, while maximally affirming his passion, gently but firmly informed him that it was becoming a "disruptive noise" to the organization.
